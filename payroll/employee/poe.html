Determine the province of employment (POE)

When employers pay employment income such as salaries, wages, or commissions, the employee's province or territory of employment (POE) must be determined so that the proper provincial/territorial income tax deductions are withheld. The province of employment is determined by whether the employee physically (in person) reports at the employer's establishment.

Income tax legislated by the province or territory

CRA's mandate is to administer provincial and territorial personal income tax as legislated by the province or territory. Any concerns regarding the tax policy of a province or territory should be directed to the applicable provincial or territorial Ministry of Finance.

What is considered the establishment of the employer

An establishment of the employer is any place or premises in Canada that is owned, leased or rented by an employer where employees report to work or from which individuals are paid. For purposes of province of employment, this does not need to be a permanent establishment.

Generally, a home office will not be considered an establishment of the employer.

Example

The place of business for a construction company can be one or more construction sites or the place of business for a carnival can include a shopping mall parking lot. The employee's province or territory of employment would be the one in which the field office or shopping mall is located.

Determine the residency status

The individual may be uncertain of their residency status.

The residency status is determined on a case by case basis and an individual's whole situation. If you want the opinion of the CRA on your residency status, complete either:

  • Form NR74, Determination of Residency Status (Entering Canada)
  • Form NR73, Determination of Residency Status (Leaving Canada)

You can send to the address on the form. Make sure to provide as many details as possible on the form.

Learn more about the residency status of an individual: Individuals – Leaving or entering Canada and non-residents

Learn more about the residency status of an employer or payer: Businesses – International and non-resident taxes

How to ask for a binding determination

To obtain certainly and require a binding determination about the tax consequences of the proposed departure from or arrival in Canada, a person may ask the Income Tax Rulings Directorate (ITRD) to issue a binding advance income tax ruling with respect to the residency status of the person. The taxpayer may have to pay a fee to have ITRD complete such a request. For more information on how to ask for an advance income tax ruling, see Information Circular IC 70-6R9, Advance Income Tax Rulings and Technical Interpretations or Income tax rulings and interpretations.

Determine the type of income

Income from employment

Employee is a resident of Canada

Situation: Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada

Use the TD1 provincial and territorial form, and the payroll deductions tables, for the province or territory where the employee reports for work.

Situation: Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer

Use the TD1 provincial and territorial form, and the payroll deductions tables, for the province or territory where the employer's establishment is located and from which the employee's salary is paid.

Situation: Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Do not use the TD1 provincial and territorial form. Use only the federal TD1 form and the payroll deductions tables for In Canada beyond the limits of any province/territory or outside Canada .

Employee reporting to more than one establishment of the employer during a pay period

Same amount of time

The province of employment is the province in which the permanent establishment of the employer is located where the employee had worked last. There is no minimum amount of time the employee has to report to that place.

Different amount of time

The province of employment is the province in which the permanent establishment of the employer is located where the employee had spent the most time. There is no minimum amount of time the employee has to report to that place.

Employee is non-resident, including a commuter

Use the TD1 provincial and territorial form, and the payroll deductions tables, for the province or territory where employment duties are performed in the following situations:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Learn more: Deemed residents of Canada.

Employee is part-year resident

If the part of the year that the employee is:

  • a resident of Canada, see Employee is a resident of Canada.
  • not a resident of Canada, see Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter.
Employee is deemed resident or sojourner

Do not use the TD1 provincial and territorial form. Use only the federal TD1 form and the payroll deductions tables for In Canada beyond the limits of any province/territory or outside Canada in the following situations:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Learn more: Deemed residents of Canada.

Non-employment income

Use the TD1 provincial and territorial form, and the payroll deductions tables, of the recipient's province or territory of residence if you pay amounts other than employment income, such as pension income, retiring allowance, or RRSP.
