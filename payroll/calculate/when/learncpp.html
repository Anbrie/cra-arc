What are CPP pensionable earnings

Generally, you must deduct CPP contributions from:

  • Salary, wages or other remuneration
  • Commissions
  • Bonuses
  • Most taxable benefits
  • Honorariums
  • Certain tips and gratuities

For the full list, go to: Determine if a special payment is taxable, pensionable or insurable.

When to deduct CPP contributions

You have to deduct CPP contributions from your employee's pensionable earnings if the employee meets all of the following conditions:

  • Employee is in pensionable employment during the year
  • Employee is not considered to be disabled under the CPP or the Quebec Pension Plan (QPP)
  • Employee is 18 to 69 years old even if the employee is receiving a CPP or QPP retirement pension

You must also contribute an amount equal to the CPP contributions that you deduct from your employees' remuneration and remit the total amount:

  • even if the required deductions were not made, you are deemed to have made them.
  • if you made an error in calculating CPP contributions

Calculation

  • Employee's CPP contributions for the pay period
  • +plus Your share of CPP contributions
  • =equals This amount is the total CPP contributions that you must remit for the pay period
Example
  • Employee's contribution is $240.40
  • +plus You share is $240.40
  • =equals Total CPP contributions to remit is $480.80

When to not deduct CPP contributions

  • Employment not subject to CPP
  • Benefits and payments not subject to CPP

Employment not subject to CPP

You do not deduct CPP contributions from any of the following employment types:

  • Casual employment for a purpose other than that of the employer's usual trade or business
  • When an employee works in agriculture or horticulture, under certain conditions
  • Employment of a spouse or common-law partner if you cannot deduct the remuneration paid as an expense under the Income Tax Act
  • Employment of your child or a person whom you maintain if no cash remuneration is paid

Benefits and payments not subject to CPP

You do not deduct CPP contributions from any of the following benefits and payments:

  • Pension payments
  • Wage loss benefits
  • Lump sum payments from a pension plan
  • Death benefits

What if the employee has different jobs

The CPP annual maximum pensionable earnings apply to each job the employee holds with different employers even if the maximum contribution has been reached during a previous employment.

  • any overpayments will be refunded to employees when they file their income tax and benefit returns
  • you are not entitled to a refund

When to start, stop or restart deducting CPP contributions

For current employees or pensioners, you need determine if you need to stop deducting CPP because the employee reached the maximum contribution for the year.

This applies to each job the employee holds with different employers (different business numbers). If an employee leaves one employer during the year to start work with another employer, the new employer also has to deduct CPP contributions without taking into account what the previous employer paid. This is the case even if the employee has contributed the maximum amount during the previous employment.

Calculation

  • Employee's maximum CPP contributions for the year
  • -minus Employee's contributions to date for the year
  • =equals This amount is the maximum CPP contribution that you can deduct from your employee's pay for the rest of the year
Example
  • $3,166.45 is Joseph's maximum contributions for the year 2021
  • -minus $300 is the amount he contributed to date for the year
  • =equals $2,866.45 is the maximum CPP amount you can deduct

Special situations

You may also have to start, stop or restart deducting CPP depending on the following situation:

  • Employee turns 18 in the year
  • Employee turns 70 in the year
  • Employee gives you a completed Form CPT30
  • Employee is considered to be disabled under the CPP
  • Employee dies in the year

What to do if you pay a former employee

If you pay an amount to a former employee and you have to deduct CPP contributions, use the current rate in effect when you make the payment.

Any overpayments will be refunded to employees when they file their income tax and benefit returns. However, there is no provision in the CPP that would allow us to refund or credit the employer for his or her contributions in those circumstances.

What to do if you have a business in Quebec

Quebec employers deduct the Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) contributions instead of CPP contributions.

Learn more: Québec Pension Plan Contributions | Revenu Québec

What to do if you transfer an employee from Quebec to another province or territory

You may have a place of business in Quebec and in another province or territory. If you transfer an employee from Quebec to another part of Canada before the end of the year, you must use a formula to reconcile the amounts contributed to the CPP and the QPP to make sure that enough contributions to the CPP are withheld and future benefits are not affected.

If you transfer an employee from Quebec to another province or territory, you can take into account the QPP contributions you deducted from that employee throughout the year when calculating the maximum CPP contributions to deduct. In addition to deducting CPP/QPP contributions and EI/QPIP premiums you will also have to prepare two T4 slips. It is important that you calculate and report the proper deductions and insurable/pensionable earnings on both T4 slips.

What to do if you are self-employed

If your net self-employment income and pensionable employment income is more than $3,500, you have to contribute to the Canada Pension Plan (CPP).

You need to calculate the amount of CPP you have to contribute when you complete your income tax and benefit return (line 22200, line 31000, line 42100, and Schedule 8).
