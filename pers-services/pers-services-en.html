Tax implications for incorporated individuals

If you're a single-person small business, incorporating could change your tax status. You may be considered an incorporated employee and no longer be able to claim certain expenses.

Determining the status of your business

An incorporated employee is someone who is:

  • registered as a corporation
  • hired by another business to perform services on behalf of the corporation
  • performs services that would normally be performed by an employee of the business

A self-employed individual performs similar functions, but is not incorporated.

An incorporated employee is also known as a personal services business.

Businesses might hire individuals to fulfill short or long-term needs. In some industries, the hiring business may require these individuals to register as a corporation.

If you register yourself as a corporation, you may be considered a personal services business by the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) if you:

  • are the only shareholder and likely the only employee of your corporation
  • perform services for a business that would usually be performed by one of its employees
  • use equipment and/or tools provided by that business to perform those services

In this case, your relationship with the hiring business is perceived as an employee/employer relationship, meaning you're considered an incorporated employee (personal services business) rather than a self-employed individual.

Income Tax Act regulations

The Income Tax Act outlines the four conditions that must be met to be considered a personal services business:

  1. The income of the corporation is from the services performed by an incorporated employee on half of the corporation
  2. The incorporated employee who is performing the services, or any person related to the incorporated employee, is a specified shareholder in the corporation
  3. If the corporation did not exist, the incorporated employee would be considered an employee of the business receiving the services
  4. The corporation does not employ more than 5 full-time employees in the business throughout the year
Example: A personal services business

John is looking for a job. An Ontario-based trucking company (ABC Trucking) offers him a 12-month contract position with full-time hours. The contract comes with the stipulation thatJohn performs his services through a corporation.

John registers as a corporation (858689 Ontario Inc.), of which he is the only shareholder and only employee. 858689 Ontario Inc.'s only client is ABC Trucking.

858689 Ontario Inc. bills ABC Trucking for the services it performs, and it receives payment from ABC Trucking for those services. 858689 Ontario Inc. either keeps the funds in the corporation or disburses them to John.

In this example, 858689 Ontario Inc. meets the four conditions outlined in the Income Tax Act to be considered a personal services business:

  1. 858689 Ontario Inc.'s income is from services performed by John (incorporated employee) for ABC Trucking
  2. John is a specified shareholder of 858689 Ontario Inc.
  3. John performs the work of an ABC Trucking employee using their trucks, and would therefore be considered an employee of ABC Trucking had he not incorporated as 858689 Ontario Inc.
  4. John is the only employee of 858689 Ontario Inc.

Difference between a personal services business and a self-employed individual

Consider these factors when differentiating between a personal services business and a self-employed individual:

  • How many clients they have at a given time
  • Whether or not they are incorporated

Personal services business

  • Only performs services for one client at a time
  • Is a registered corporation
Example: A personal services business

Helen is an accountant and operates her own accounting business. She registered as a corporation (Helen Oscar Accounting Ltd.), and is the only shareholder and the only employee. She recently finished a 12-month contract with ABC Cars, a major car dealer in town, working in-house as their accountant. She now has another 12-month contract, this time with the 123 Cars. However, she only works from their main office a couple of days a week.

In this example, Helen Oscar Accounting Ltd. meets the four conditions outlined in the Income Tax Act to be considered a personal services business:

  1. Helen Oscar Accounting Ltd.'s income is from services performed by Helen (incorporated employee) for one company at a time.
  2. Helen is a specified shareholder of Helen Oscar Accounting Ltd.
  3. Helen performs the work of an employee using their equipment, office and supplies, and would therefore be considered an employee of the individual car dealerships had she not incorporated as Helen Oscar Accounting Ltd.
  4. Helen is the only employee of Helen Oscar Accounting Ltd.

Self-employed individual

  • Generally performs services for several clients at the same time
  • Is not incorporated
  • Usually considered a sole proprietor or independent contractor
Example: A personal services business

Jane removes snow from private properties during the winter. She operates her own business, which is not incorporated, and has several clients throughout the city. She can work when she wants, as long as her clients' needs are taken care of

In this example, Jane is a self-employed individual (specially, a sole proprietor). She is not incorporated, performs services for several clients at the same time, and does not meet the conditions outlined in the Income Tax Act to be considered a personal services business.

For more information, see Guide RC4110, Employee or self-employed?

Tax deductions, tax rates, and eligible expenses for a personal services business

If you're an incorporated individual and considered a personal services business by the CRA, your tax obligations are different from other corporations

Tax deductions and tax rates

A personal services business is subject to the full federal and provincial corporate tax rates on all taxable income, plus an additional 5%

Unlike other corporations, a personal services business is not eligible for the:

  • general tax reduction
  • small business deduction

Eligible expenses

A personal services business can only deduct the following expenses

  • any salary, wages, benefit, or allowance the corporation pays to its incorporated employee(s)
  • legal expenses the corporation incurs for collecting amounts owing

Ineligible expenses

A personal services business cannot deduct most standard businesses expenses, including, but not limited to:

  • office supplies
  • travel expenses
  • meals
  • cell phone costs

Filing and payment obligations

A personal services business is required to file a T2 Corporation Income Tax Return and pay any amounts owing to the CRA by the corporate tax filing and payment deadlines, like other corporations.

When completing the T2 return, however, it is important to remember that a personal services business is not eligible to claim the same tax deductions and expenses that are available to other corporations, and they are subject to a higher tax rate.

For details on how to complete the T2 return, refer to Guide T2012, T2 Corporation, Income Tax Guide 2019.

A personal services business is also responsible for their corporation's GST/HST withholding, filing, and remitting obligations, as well as issuing T4 slips for any employees of their corporation.

Correcting tax returns

If you believe you may be a personal services business and would like to correct your tax return(s) you previously filed, you can contact the CRA's Volantary Disclosure Program to determine if you meet the eligibility requirements and submit an application.
