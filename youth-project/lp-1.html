Overview

This lesson will provide students insight into what to expect when they start a new job. Students will be able to test their knowledge through quizzes and practical examples.

Time Frame

TBD

Core question

What happens when I start a new job?

Learning objective

Students will develop an understanding of what is required to start working in Canada. They will also learn what payroll deductions are and how they are calculated. As such, they will be better able to conceptualize how they pay income tax and contribute to social programs through employment.

Skills to develop

  • Identify the need for a social insurance number
  • Explain the purpose of a TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return
  • Describe why deductions are taken from their pay
  • Make connections between their bi-weekly pay, social programs, and their tax return

Background

85% of Canadians work as employees, as opposed to being self-employed or running a business. In Canada's tax system, these individuals will have income tax and other contributions to social programs taken directly off their pay by their employer.

Warm up exercises

First exercise

Step 1: Explain to students that whether they are starting their first job or have been working for many years, the basic components of starting a new job do not change.

Step 2: Ask students if they have already worked for an employer or a company, as opposed to babysitting or odd jobs. For those that answer yes, ask what they remember about being hired.

Suggested answers could be:

  • Providing their social insurance number
  • Filling out tax forms
  • Giving their banking information for direct deposit

Second exercise

Step 1: Inform students that one of the most common taxes that we pay in Canada is income tax. For the majority of individuals, this is taken directly off of their pay by their employer. This is known as a payroll deduction.

Step 2: Ask students if they know of any other amounts that are deducted from their pay.

Suggested answers could be:

  • CPP/QPP contributions
  • EI premiums
  • Québec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP) premiums
  • Union dues
  • Health plan premiums
  • Private pension plan contributions

Step 3: Allow students a few minutes to write down why they think payroll deductions are important. Ask them to keep these in mind as they review the next lesson pages.

Learning and activities

Read through the lesson pages with the additional guidance provided below. Students can review material individually, in groups, or as a class.  

Lesson 1: Why you need a SIN or tax number

Activity: Advise students that all individuals in Canada require a SIN to work or to receive benefits and services from the government. Depending on their residency status and their citizenship status, they may receive a permanent or a temporary SIN. An individual's number usually stays with them indefinitely.

Activity: Stress to students the importance of keeping their SIN safe. Discuss the implications of a compromised SIN, such as fraud or identity theft, and the long term impacts that this can have.

Lesson 2: When to fill out a TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return

Activity: Inform students that they will need complete a TD1 whenever they start a new job. As discussed previously, their employer will take income tax off of their pay. The TD1 allows their employer to complete this calculation accurately based on their personal tax situation.

Activity: Explain that, in Canada, the income tax that they owe on their income is calculated when they do their taxes, using information like their taxable income and the tax credits they're entitled to. Their employer will, ideally, withhold enough tax to cover the taxes they owe on their income at the end of the year. This is why it's important to complete the TD1 accurately: to ensure that the correct amount of tax is withheld – no more, no less.

Tax credits

Activity: Display the federal TD1 to the class and review the list of personal tax credits that can be claimed. Ask students which tax credits they think they might be able to claim, either currently or in future years.

Lesson 3: What is on your pay stub

Activity: Discuss the concept of payroll with students. Explain that their employer will first calculate their pay based on the hours they work and their wage, and will then calculate what amounts to take off. As previously mentioned, the most common deduction is income tax, which is calculated based on their income and the TD1 that they submit. Other amounts will also be deducted, such as CPP/QPP contributions and EI premiums, and these amounts are calculated as a pre-determined percentage of their income. Advise that their employer will make a matching contribution to these programs on their behalf.

Activity: Watch the Understanding your pay stub video in part 3 of 5 of the lesson. Ask students to look back at the notes they took during the warm up and to compare them to what they learned in the video. Share some of their ideas about why payroll deductions are important. Highlight that their contributions to social programs make these programs possible and help fund the future payments they may receive from these programs. Inform students that they can also claim tax credits for these amounts when they do their taxes.

Activity: Ask students what would happen if their employer did not take income tax off of their pay, and what this might mean for individuals earning income through odd jobs where tax is not automatically deducted, such as babysitting or mowing the lawn. Inform them that the full amount of taxes they owe on their income would need to be paid when they do their taxes, as opposed to paying it gradually over the course of the year.

Lesson 4: What is on your T4

Activity: Advise students that the T4 slip is one of the most common documents they will use when they do their taxes. It provides a history of their employment income and deductions for the entire year, similar to the “year-to-date” information found on their paystub.

Bring it all together

Direct students to complete the quiz to assess their understanding.

Discuss the connection between their employment earnings and the tax return that they will complete every year:

  • Use their SIN to identify themselves with the CRA when they do their taxes
  • Have the option of claiming the tax credits shown on the TD1 on their tax return
  • Report the employment income they earned, as the taxes that they may owe are calculated using their income
  • Claim tax credits for certain payroll deductions, such as CPP/QPP contributions and EI premiums, to help lower the taxes they owe
  • Use their pay stubs to confirm the accuracy of their T4 slip. They will then use their T4 to report their earnings when they do their taxes

Reconfirm that the information learned will be useful to students when they start to work in Canada, but that the same concepts will still apply once they have been working for many years.
