Identify users' keywords

Determine the user's keywords by looking at the site analytics. Additionally, Google Trends will show search term frequency by country, province, timeframe, and category.

Examples:

  • In the 12 months, searches in Canada for "wellness" out-performs "well-being". The daily average is:
    • 84 searches for "wellness"
    • 1 search for "well-being"
  • In the 12 months, searches in Canada for "procurement" is similar to searches for "purchasing". However:
    1. Scroll down the page to get more information.
    2. Change the drop-down "Rising" to "Top" to see the context of how procurement is most frequently searched.
      Before
      After
    3. Notice the context of when "procurement" is being searched. The third most popular "procurement" search is "procurement meaning", which suggests that many users don't know what it is in the first place. So although "procurement" is a popular term, it is not necessarily popular for the right reason.

Determining a user's keywords is important, but be sure to also determine why it's popular. After a thorough analysis, popular keywords must be considered when creating content.

Get additional data sources from colleagues

There are many sources of data available when you are designing content.

Online
  • web analytics to find out how often people visit the page, how much time they spend on a page and where they go from there
  • search terms and searches from the page can tell you what people expect to find, but can’t web page feedback collected on each page
  • how people are using Canada.ca - basic data
  • Government of Canada 100 top tasks inventory
  • social listening - using your topic keywords to browse conversations on social networks and forums like Twitter, Facebook, Reddit
  • metadata audit tool to find any problems related to how content is found in search
Call-in
  • call centre data
  • complaints and feedback received from the public
Engagment sessions
  • Public Opinion Research studies and other research studies funded by your organization; some reports such as adults and evaluation reports are available on Canada.ca institutional pages, for example Canada Revenue Agency reports)
  • user research and usability testing done by your or other government organizations
  • data from content improvement projects on Canada.ca

Testing your content

Depending on what you want to find out, there are different ways to test content.

You can test content for:

  • readability and plain language
  • understanding
  • accessibility
  • task completion

Some common approaches to test your content with users are:

Cloze test
ask people to fill in the blanks with words they think are missing
First-click test
ask people where they would go to find specific information on a website to determine if they can find it on first try
Confidence test
ask people to read a piece of content and highlight in different colours what they understand, find confusing, don’t understand and didn’t read
A/B test
show people two versions of the same content with one small change and ask them what they understood (there are also other ways of doing A/B tests)
5 second test
ask people to read a piece of content and ask them to tell you what they understood after 5 seconds
Card sorting
ask people to group a number of topics under predetermined categories or ask people to group a number of topics under the categories they get to name

All of these methods can be done

  • in-person and remotely
  • quickly and inexpensively

Have a research team? Work with them to set up a usability test.

Don’t have access to professional tools or a research team who can support you? Don’t worry. You don’t need special software to do these tests. Be creative. Use paper prototyping or common collaboration tools like Google docs, slides or Trello to test content.

Don’t have access to representative users? Ask colleagues who don’t know the subject to participate in tests.

Be careful about collecting or keeping any personal data while testing content.

You can also use automated tools to:

  • check content structure
  • check the reading level of content

Interpreting data

Data can sometimes be difficult to interpret. To make sure you are making accurate and unbiased interpretations of data:

  • Use multiple sources of data to understand the problem
  • Combine qualitative (what people say) and quantitative data (insights represented as numbers)
  • Ask for help from user researchers and data analysts in your organization
  • Invite different team members to contribute their diverse readings of data
