What are EI premiums earnings

Generally, you have to deduct EI premiums from:

  • Salary, wages or other remuneration
  • Commissions
  • Bonuses
  • Most taxable benefits
  • Honorariums
  • Certain tips and gratuities

For the full list, go to: Determine if a special payment is taxable, pensionable or insurable.

When to deduct EI premiums

Employers and their employees have to pay EI premiums on gratuities and remuneration from insurable employment.

EI premiums:

  • are payable from the first dollar earned up to the yearly maximum
  • have no age limit for deducting them

You must also contribute an amount equal to the EI premiums that you deduct from your employees' remuneration and remit the total amount:

  • even if the required deductions were not made, you are deemed to have made them
  • if you made an error in calculating EI premiums

Insurable employment includes most employment in Canada under a contract of service.

Arm's length employment

Arm's length employment is when the terms and conditions of the employment are substantially similar to what they would be no matter who was hired for the position on the open job market.

Hiring a family member or a related person

Family members and related persons may be not at arm's length if they are related to their employer.

Learn more: Determine if a special situation is taxable, pensionable or insurable

When to not deduct EI premiums

  • Employment not subject to EI
  • Benefits and payments not subject to EI

Employment not subject to EI

You do not deduct EI premiums from any of the following employment types:

  • Casual employment for a purpose other than that of the employer's usual trade or business
  • When an employee works in agriculture or horticulture, under certain conditions
  • Employment when the employer and the employee do not deal with each other at arm's length
  • When a corporation employs a person who controls more than 40% of the corporation's voting shares

Benefits and payments not subject to EI

You do not deduct EI premiums from any of the following benefits and payments:

  • Retiring allowances
  • Monies earned before the death of an employee but not yet paid at the time of death
  • Employer contributions to an employee's group RRSP under certain conditions

What to do when there is an interruption of earnings

You are required to provide your employee with a record of employment (ROE) when there is an interruption of earnings. An interruption of earnings occurs when the employment ends or an employee leaves because of pregnancy, injury, illness, adoption leave, layoff, leave without pay, or dismissal.

The information on the ROE is used by the Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) to determine EI eligibility.

Learn more: Employee leaving

What if the employee has different jobs

The EI annual maximum insurable earnings apply to each job the employee holds with different employers even if the maximum premium has been reached during the previous employment.

  • any overpayments will be refunded to employees when they file their income tax and benefit returns
  • you are not entitled to a refund

If your business went through a restructure or reorganization, refer to: Make changes to your account

What happens if you pay a former employee

If you pay an amount to a former employee and you have to deduct EI premiums, use the current rate in effect when you make the payment.

Any overpayments will be refunded to employees when they file their income tax and benefit returns. However, there is no provision in the EI program that would allow us to refund or credit you the contributions in those circumstances.

What to do if you have a business in Quebec


If you have employees working in Quebec, regardless of your employee's province or territory of residence, you have to deduct a reduced employment insurance (EI) premium using the Quebec EI premium rates and maximums in addition to the QPIP premiums.

Learn more: Québec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP) Premiums | Revenu Québec

What to do if you transfer an employee from Quebec to another province or territory

You may have a place of business in Quebec and one in another province or territory. If you transfer an employee from Quebec to another province or territory, in addition to deducting CPP/QPP contributions and EI/QPIP premiums you will also have to prepare two T4 slips.

It is important that you calculate and report the proper deductions and insurable/pensionable earnings on both T4 slips.

What to do if you are self-employed

If you are self-employed, the participation in the EI program is voluntary.

You are eligible to participate if all of the following apply:

  • You do not work as a barber, hairdresser, fisher, taxi driver, or a driver of other passenger vehicles
  • You are a Canadian citizen or a permanent resident of Canada.
  • You either operate your own business or work for and control more than 40% of the voting shares of a corporation

If you are a barber, a hairdresser, a fisher, a taxi driver, or a driver of other passenger vehicles barber, If you are in these professions, you should apply for EI benefits as an employee.

Learn more: EI benefits for self-employed people

What is the EI Premium Reduction Program

If you provide a wage loss replacement plan for short-term disability to your employees, you can request a reduced EI premium rate through Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC).

If approved you will have a reduced EI premium rate that is less than the standard 1.4 times the employee premium (for example, 1.24 times).

Your reduced rate only applies to employees who are covered by the approved plan.

In this situation, you will require an additional payroll deduction program account to make separate remittances for employees not covered by the plan.
