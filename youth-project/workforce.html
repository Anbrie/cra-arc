Why you need a SIN or tax number (part 1 of 3)

What is a Social Insurance Number (SIN)

A social insurance number, often referred to as a SIN , is a nine-digit identification number that all individuals need to work in Canada. Your SIN will allow you to receive benefits and services from the Government of Canada. The federal organization that issues SINs is Service Canada.

For citizens and permanent residents of Canada, the first digit of their SIN is typically associated with their province or territory of residence when they first applied for their SIN. Your SIN does not expire. You may be issued a new SIN only if there is proof that your original SIN was used fraudulently.

Example: Receiving and using a SIN

Jasmine was a resident of Nova Scotia when they applied for their SIN. Their SIN is 111-111-111. The first digit confirms where their SIN was issued. Jasmine will keep using this SIN, even if they move to another province or territory for school or work.

A temporary SIN is issued to visitors, permit holders, refugees, and temporary workers who are legally entitled to work in Canada. These numbers begin with a 9 and have an expiry date.

What if you are not eligible for a SIN?

Individuals who are considered non-residents, such as international students, may not be able to get a SIN but still have to file an income tax and benefit return. These individuals may apply for an individual tax number (ITN) through the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA). An ITN starts with 09.

Residents of Canada who are not entitled to receive a SIN or an ITN and still have to file a return may be able to apply for a temporary tax number (TTN) through the CRA. A TTN starts with either 01 or 03.

If you need to apply for an ITN or a TTN, contact the CRA.

Getting a SIN

You may already have a SIN. In Canada, parents will usually apply for their child’s SIN when they are born. This is done through the Newborn Registration Service.

Definition for: Newborn Registration Service
Newborn registration Service

If you do not already have a SIN, you can apply for one online, by mail, or in person through Service Canada.

If you are not sure if you have a SIN, or have forgotten or lost it, visit a Service Canada office to confim if you have a SIN and retrieve it.

Keep your SIN safe

Your SIN is unique, personal, and confidential. You are the only person who is supposed to use it, and you are responsible for keeping it safe.

If you lose your SIN, this could lead to fraud or even identity theft.

Tip

Memorize your SIN, and keep it in a safe place instead of carrying it with you.

You must give your SIN to your employer when you start a job and to your financial institution when you open an account. The employer and the institution need your SIN in order to send information about your income to the CRA. The employer and the institution are also responsible for protecting your personal information.

You also need to provide your SIN to a government agency or department to access a benefit or service. This includes providing your SIN to the CRA when you file your income tax and benefit return, because the SIN identifies you in the CRA’s system.

Example: Using your SIN when you start a new job

Omar has had a SIN their whole life because their parents applied for it when Omar was born. The parents received a letter from Service Canada confirming Omar’s SIN, and the parents keep the letter safely in a filing cabinet.

Last year, Omar was hired at their first job and needed to complete some paperwork before starting. Some of the forms required Omar to enter their SIN. Omar knew that this was safe to do.

In February of this year, Omar’s employer sent information about Omar’s income to the CRA. Omar will use the same information, along with their SIN, when they file their income tax and benefit return by the April deadline.

When to fill out a TD1 Personal Tax Credits Return (part 1 of 3)

What is the purpose of a TD1 form

Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return, must be completed when you start a new job so that your employer can pay you accurately.

One of the most common taxes that you pay in Canada is income tax. If you are an employee, your employer is responsible for deducting this tax from your pay.

When you start a new job, you will fill out a TD1 form with your best estimate of your tax situation. Your employer uses this form to calculate the amount of income tax to deduct from your pay.

Filling out a TD1 form

The first page of a TD1 form requires you to enter personal information, such as your name, address, date of birth, and social insurance number.

You will also enter an amount or amounts such as the basic personal amount for tax credits you will claim when you file your income tax and benefit return for the year. The tax credits will help to reduce the tax that you might owe on your income. If you are claiming other amounts, such as for tuition, your employer can deduct less tax from your paycheques. If you do not claim any other amounts, your tax credits will only be for the basic personal amount, which all residents of Canada can claim.

For more information about the different tax credits that you may be able to claim, resource links will be available after you finish this lesson.

Common situations

The second page of a TD1 form requires you to provide more information about your circumstances. The following are some of the most common situations.

Working for more than one employer at the same time

You will be asked to fill out a TD1 form every time your start a job with an employer. If you work for more than one employer at the same time, you can only claim amounts such as the basic personal amount on one of the TD1 forms you fill out. Otherwise, your employer will not deduct enough tax from the income they pay you compared to your total income. This could mean that you end up owing a lot of money when you file your income tax and benefit return.

Definition for: Basic Personal Amount (BPA)
Basic Personal Amount (BPA)

If you have more than one employer at the same time and you filled out a TD1 form claiming amounts, you cannot claim them again for the other employer in the same tax year. In that case, you should enter 0 on the first page of the latest TD1, and tick the box “More than one employer at the same time” on the second page.

Total income less than total claim amount

If your total income from all sources will be less than your total claim amount, you may not owe any tax. In this situation, your employer does not need to deduct tax from your pay.

Tick the box “Total income less than total claim amount” on the second page of the TD1 form.

Additional tax to be deducted

If you receive other income where tax is not taken off, such as cash tips or occasional earnings, you may want your employer to deduct more tax from your pay. This means that you’ll pay a little bit more tax each time you are paid rather than possibly having to pay a larger amount when you file your income tax and benefit return. Any amount that you overpay will be refunded to you after the CRA processes your return.

Enter the extra amount you want the employer to deduct on the second page.

You may also need to complete a provincial or territorial tax credits form for your province or territory of employment. For more information about the TD1 and the provincial and territorial forms, resource links will be available after you finish the lesson.

You must sign and date the TD1 form and give it to your employer.

Fast fact

If you work for the same employer from year to year, you do not have to complete a TD1 form every year, unless there is a change to your federal, provincial, or territorial personal tax credit amounts.
