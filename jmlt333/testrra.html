Successor holder

If named as the successor holder, the survivor (spouse or common-law partner) will become the new holder of the TFSA when the original holder dies. A survivor may also designate a successor holder. If the successor holder already has their own TFSA, they would be considered to be the holder of 2 separate accounts.

This means the TFSA can continue to exist, and its value, both at the date of the original holder’s death and any income earned after that date, are sheltered from tax implications. When the successor holder has ownership, they can make tax free withdrawals and new contributions, depending on their own unused TFSA contribution room. They could also transfer part or all of the value from one account to the other (a qualifying transfer) and it would not affect available TFSA contribution room.

Designated beneficiaries

If designated as a beneficiary, you will not have to pay tax on payments made out of the TFSA account, as long as the total payments do not exceed the fair market value (FMV) of the amount held at the time of the holder’s death. Beneficiaries can contribute any of the amounts they receive to their own TFSA as long as they have unused TFSA contribution room available.

Qualified donees

If a qualified donee (registered charities, municipalities, etc), is named as a beneficiary, the transfer of funds must generally occur within the 36-month period following the holder’s death. Once the donation has been completed, it is possible to have the deceased’s final return reassessed, to be able to claim the charitable donation tax credit.
