Sales of good located in Canada

On this page

  • What types of supplies are taxed
  • Who charges and collects GST/HST
  • When to start charging and collecting
  • Notification and information reporting
  • What rate to charge
  • What to do with collected GST/HST

Once you have registered for the standard GST/HST, you are required to begin charging and collecting the tax on the taxable supplies that you make in Canada. The rate of tax that you are required to charge and collect on those supplies is based on the place of supply that determines whether a supply is made in Canada, and the province in which a supply is made.

What types of supplies are taxed

Tax is charged and collected on qualifying sales of goods that are made to purchasers.

A qualifying sale of a good is generally a taxable sale of a good that is to be delivered or made available to the purchaser in Canada. It does not include a sale of a good that is to be sent by mail or courier to the purchaser at an address in Canada from an address outside Canada by the supplier or by another person acting on behalf of the supplier. This would therefore apply to the sale of goods that are located in Canada, such as in a fulfillment warehouse, or shipped from a place in Canada to a purchaser in Canada.

Although the calculation of the registration threshold under this measure is based on qualifying sales of goods to specified recipients, the obligation to charge and collect tax under the measure applies to qualifying sales of goods made to any purchaser.

Place of supply regime for qualifying sales of goods

The place of supply regime that will determine the rate of GST/HST that you are required to charge and collect on qualifying sales of goods are the normal GST/HST place of supply regime for sales of goods.

A qualifying sale of goods will be made in a particular province if the goods are delivered or made available to the purchaser in the province.

In this case, goods will be considered to be delivered in a province if the supplier:

  • mails or sends the goods by courier to an address in the province;
  • ships the goods to a destination in the province that is specified in the contract for their carriage; or
  • transfers possession of the goods to a common carrier or consignee that the supplier has retained on behalf of the recipient to ship the goods to a destination in the province that is specified in the contract for their carriage.
Example 1 - Sale of goods made through distribution platform

A registered distribution platform operator operates a distribution platform through which it facilitates the sale of goods by vendors from around the world. The operator has fulfillment warehouses that are located throughout Canada from which it fulfills orders for goods sold by the vendors.

A non-resident vendor who is not registered for GST/HST and is not carrying on business in Canada has some of its taxable goods in one of the fulfillment warehouses that is located in Ontario. The non-resident vendor sells one of the goods through the platform to a consumer who lives in Ontario. The platform operator subsequently sends the good by courier to the address of the consumer in Ontario from its fulfillment warehouse.

A Canadian vendor in Ontario who is not registered for GST/HST also has some of its taxable goods in one of the fulfillment warehouses that is located in Ontario. The Canadian vendor sells one of the goods through the platform to a consumer who lives in Nova Scotia. The platform operator subsequently sends the good by courier to the address of the consumer in Nova Scotia from its fulfillment warehouse.

The supply of the good to the consumer in Ontario is made in Ontario since it is sent by courier to an address in that province. The distribution platform operator is therefore required to charge and collect HST at the rate of 13% on the sale of the good. The HST is required to be calculated on the final sale price of the good to the consumer that is charged by the non-resident vendor.

The supply of the good to the consumer in Nova Scotia is made in Nova Scotia since it is sent by courier to an address in that province. The distribution platform operator is therefore required to charge and collect HST at the rate of 15% on the sale of the good. The HST is required to be calculated on the final sale price of the good to the consumer that is charged by the Canadian vendor.

Example 2 - Sale of goods made directly by registered non-resident vendor

A non-resident vendor who has registered for GST/HST as required under this proposed measure sells taxable goods through its website. The vendor has some of its goods in a fulfillment warehouse that is located in British Columbia. The non-resident vendor sells one of the goods to a consumer who lives in British Columbia. The good is subsequently sent by courier to the address of the consumer in British Columbia.

The supply of the good is made in British Columbia since it is sent by courier to an address in that province. The vendor is therefore required to charge and collect GST at the rate of 5% on the sale of the good to the consumer. The HST is required to be calculated on the final sale price of the good to the consumer that is charged by vendor.

Example 3 - Sale of goods made directly by non-registered non-resident vendor

A non-resident vendor who is not registered for GST/HST and is not carrying on business in Canada only sells taxable goods through its website. The vendor does not have goods that are stored in Canada. The vendor sells a good for $100 US to a consumer who lives in Alberta and sends the good by courier from outside Canada to the address of the consumer in Alberta.

The vendor is not required to collect GST/HST on the supply of the good. The supply is made outside Canada since the vendor is not registered for GST/HST and is not carrying on business in Canada. The vendor is also not subject to the proposed measure for qualifying sales of goods that are located in Canada. The GST would apply to the importation of the good at a rate of 5%.

Who charges and collects GST/HST

The person who is required to charge and collect GST/HST under the proposed measure on qualifying sales of goods to purchasers depends on if:

  • those supplies are made directly by the vendor or are facilitated by a distribution platform through its platform
  • the vendor is registered for GST/HST under the normal GST/HST registration regime

Vendors

If you are a vendor who is registered under the normal GST/HST regime, you would be required to charge and collect GST/HST on your qualifying sales of goods. This includes those sales that are facilitated by a distribution platform operator through its platform.

Distribution platform operators

If you are a distribution platform operator (resident or not), you would be required to charge and collect GST/HST on qualifying sales of goods that you facilitate through your platform and that are made by vendors who are not registered for GST/HST (resident or not). You would be required to charge and collect GST/HST on the final sale price for these goods that is charged to the purchasers by the vendors.

You would not be required to charge and collect GST/HST on the platform services that you provide to these non-registered vendors in relation to these goods.

As a distribution platform operator, you would also be required to charge and collect GST/HST on your own qualifying sales of goods that you make directly to purchasers.

Generally, under this measure a specified distribution platform is a digital platform through which a person facilitates the making of qualifying sales of goods by another person who is not registered under the normal GST/HST registration regime. A digital platform generally includes a website, an electronic portal, gateway, store or distribution platform or any other similar electronic interface, but does not include an electronic interface that solely processes payments.

When to start charging and collecting

You are required to begin charging and collecting the tax under the standard GST/HST on the taxable supplies that you make in Canada as soon as you become registered

Disclosing the tax to customers

For information about charging and collecting GST/HST under the normal GST/HST regime, go to Receipts and Invoices.

Reporting period

You will be required to file a GST/HST return for each reporting period to report your net tax for the reporting period.

For more information about the rules for filing for the standard GST/HST, see Due dates for filing a GST/HST return

Net tax calculation

Your net tax calculation under this proposed measure will be determined based on the standard methods for calculating your net tax.

However, as a registered distribution platform operator you would also be eligible to claim input tax credits in respect of the tax paid on the importation of goods by non-registered vendors who import their goods into Canada to be sold through your platform. In order to so, you would be required to obtain satisfactory evidence from the vendor that the tax in respect of the importation has been paid.

For more information on how to file a tax return, see Complete and file a return

Notification and information reporting

Storage service providers - Notification and record keeping

You will have an obligation to notify us if you are a business, such as a fulfillment warehouse, that provides storage services in respect of goods that are in Canada, and that are offered for sale by non-resident vendors. You will be required to provide us with this notification:

  • by January 1, 2022, if you supply the storage services in the course of a business carried on as of July 1, 2021; and
  • in any other case, within six months after the day on which you last began supplying the storage services in the course of a business, or any later day that we may allow.

This web page will be updated once the form and manner in which the notification is to be provided are determined.

You will also be required to maintain records containing certain information in respect of these storage services that will be specified once it has been determined.

Distribution platform operators - Information return

If you are a distribution platform operator in respect of a qualifying tangible personal property supply made in a calendar year, you will be required to file an information return year containing information:

  • by the day that is six months after the end of the calendar year; or
  • by any later day that we may allow.

This web page will be updated once the information return is completed and the information that it is to contain is determined.

What rate to charge

The rate of tax that you are required to charge and collect on qualifying sales of goods depends on they are made in Canada, and they are made in a participating province.

The current rates of tax are:

  • 5% (GST) in Alberta, British Columbia, Manitoba, Northwest Territories, Nunavut, Quebec, Saskatchewan, and Yukon
  • 13% (HST) in Ontario
  • 15% (HST) in New Brunswick, Newfoundland and Labrador, Nova Scotia, and Prince Edward Island

The rates are the same for the simplified and standard GST/HST.

What to do with collected GST/HST

You are responsible to hold the GST/HST in trust until you send it to the CRA. This includes the collectible tax that you charged and have not collected yet.

At the end of each reporting period, you need to:

  • Complete and file a GST/HST return
  • Remit (pay) the GST/HST you collected
