Understand what CPP contribution tables are

CPP contribution tables are [description here].

Use when

  • The payment is made with regular pay
  • You received Form TD1-IN (in addition to the TD1) from your employee, only use these tables on the portion of their income which is taxable with the claims information reported on the TD1

Do not use when

  • The payment (for example, bonuses, commissions, retroactive pay or other types of regular payments) is made separately from regular pay
  • The payment is for deductions in a single period, multiple periods, or year-end verification
  • The employee reached the CPP maximum contributions for the year
  • You received Form TD1X from your employee
  • You received Form TD3F from the individual, refer to: Fishers and employment insurance

The annual basic exemption  is built into the CPP tables.

Calculate CPP on regular payments

Reminder: There are automated solutions that will calculate the CPP deductions for you.

Steps

  1. Determine if you can use CPP contribution tables

    CPP contribution tables can be used in most situations. Refer to: When to use CPP contribution tables 

  2. Determine if you need to stop deducting CPP

    For current employees or pensioners, determine if they have reached the maximum contribution for the year .

    If they have reach the maximum, refer to: Manual CPP calculation

    If they have not reached the maximum, proceed to the next step.

  3. Get the CPP contributions tables

    Use the CPP contributions tables  that matches your pay period.

  4. Determine the date range

    Find the range that includes your employee's gross remuneration in the "Pay" column, include any taxable benefits

  5. Get the amount of CPP contributions to deduct

    Find the amount in the "CPP" column which is next to the the column "Pay"

  6. Result: Amount of CPP contributions to deduct and remit

    This amount is the CPP contribution that you should withhold from your employee's pay.

    You have to remit this amount and the same amount for your share of the CPP contribution.

Calculate CPP on bonuses, retroactive payments or irregular amounts

In addition to the regular types of payments, you must deduct CPP contributions from bonuses, retroactive payments or other types of irregular payments.

When to use the bonus method

  • Use the bonus method

    If the payment is made separately from regular pay, use the bonus method to calculate manually because no basic exemption  is included

  • Do not use the bonus method

    If the payment is made with regular pay, use: Payroll Deductions Online Calculator (PDOC) or Calculations of CPP, EI and income tax deductions using the tax tables

Why use this bonus method

This method will result in a tax deduction that better estimates the amount of tax the individual will owe on the employment income when filing their personal income tax return. Using the bonus method provides a fairer tax deduction because the payment is spread over the year and taxed accordingly.

    If they have reach the maximum, refer to: Manual CPP calculation

Manual CPP calculation

[content]

Reminder: There are automated solutions that will calculate the CPP deductions for you.

  1. Determine if you need to stop deducting CPP

    For current employees or pensioners, determine if they have reached the maximum contribution for the year .

    If they have reach the maximum, do not deduct CPP contributions.

    If they have not reached the maximum, proceed to the next step.

  2. Calculate the basic pay-period exemption

    Use the basic exemption amount  that matches your pay period.

  3. Calculate the total pensionable income

    Calculation

    • Employee's gross pay for the period
    • +plus Employee's taxable benefits and allowances for the period
    • -minus Income from employment, benefits, and payments from which you do not deduct CPP contributions
    • =equals This is the total pensionable income.

  4. Deduct the basic pay-period exemption from the total pensionable income

    Deduct the basic pay-period exemption in step 1 from the total pensionable income for the period in step 2.

  5. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions

    Multiply the result of step 3 by the current year’s CPP contribution rate (5.10% for 2019). Make sure you do not exceed the maximum for the year. The result is the amount of contributions you should deduct from the employee.

  6. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to pay

    As an employer, you have to pay the same amount as your employee. Multiply the result of step 4 by 2.

    7. Manual calculation examples

    Joseph receives a weekly salary of $500 and $50 in taxable benefits. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions that you have to pay.

    Step 1: Calculate the basic pay-period exemption

    $3,500 ÷ 52 = $67.30 (do not round off)

    Step 2: Calculate the total pensionable income

    $500 + $50 = $550

    Step 3: Deduct the basic pay-period exemption from the total pensionable income

    $550 – $67.30 = $482.70

    Step 4: Calculate the amount of CPP contributions

    $482.70 × 5.45% = $26.30

    Step 5: Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to pay

    $26.30 × 2 = $52.60
