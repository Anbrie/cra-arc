What are the reasonable automobile allowance rates

The automobile allowance rates for 2022 are:

  • 61¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
  • 55¢ per kilometre driven after that
    In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

The automobile allowance rates for 2021 are:

  • 59¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
  • 53¢ per kilometre driven after that
    In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

The automobile allowance rates for 2020 are:

  • 59¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
  • 53¢ per kilometre driven after that
    In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

The automobile allowance rates for 2019 are:

  • 58¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
  • 52¢ per kilometre driven after that
    In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

The automobile allowance rates for 2018 are:

  • 55¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
  • 49¢ per kilometre driven after that
    In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

The automobile allowance rates for 2016 and 2017 are:

  • 54¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
  • 48¢ per kilometre driven after that<
    In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

The automobile allowance rates for 2015 are:

  • 55¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
  • 49¢ per kilometre driven after that
    In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

If the benefit is taxable, you must withhold the following deductions. The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

The withholding and remitting requirement depends on the type of remuneration: cash , non-cash , or near-cash .

  • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

    Withhold:

    • Income tax
    • CPP
    • EI (do not withhold)

    Remit:

    • GST/HST

  • Cash: Option 2

    Withhold:

    • Income tax
    • CPP
    • EI

    Remit:

    • GST/HST - Reimbursements
    • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

Report the benefit on a slip

If the benefit is taxable, you must report the following on the T4 slip.

  • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

    Report on:

    • Box 14 - Employment Income
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • Code 40 - Other Information

  • Cash: Option 2

    Report on:

    • Box 14 - Employment Income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • Code 40 - Other Information

Your eligible expenses for qualifying properties you rent are different from those for qualifying properties that you own.

Do you rent or own this property?

Eligible expenses if you rent a qualifying property
  • Rent (including rent based on a percentage of sales, profit or similar criteria)
  • Amounts required to be paid by you under a net lease (either to the lessor or a third party).
  • Operating expenses that might be customarily charged to a tenant under a net lease and which may qualify for a rent subsidy generally relate to operating the qualifying property, not to operating the tenant’s business.
  • These required amounts might include:
    • base rent
    • regular payments for customary operating expenses, such as
      • property insurance
      • utilities
      • common area maintenance
    • property and similar taxes, including school and municipal taxes
    • regular payments to the lessor for customary ancillary services
  • Example: Eligible net lease expenses

    Tenant A is an eligible entity for the purpose of a rent subsidy. They operate a retail store in a shopping mall.

    Tenant A leases its premises under a net lease with Landlord B. The lease was entered into before October 9, 2020. Tenant A and Landlord B deal at arm’s length.

    Under the net lease, Landlord B provides all of the tenants with the following services for the shopping mall:

    • heating, air conditioning and ventilation (HVAC)
    • electricity
    • water

    Landlord B also pays the property taxes and insurance for the shopping mall.

    The lease states that Landlord B will not provide phone or internet service for the tenants and the tenant is responsible for paying their phone and internet costs.

    The net lease requires that each tenant pay their share of the HVAC, electricity, water, property taxes and insurance (the “property expenses”). Landlord B calculates this amount and invoices each tenant for their property expenses each month.

    The net lease states that each month, Tenant A is required to pay:

    • a base rent and
    • its share of the property expenses according to the invoice they receive from Landlord B.

    These payments are eligible rent expenses for Tenant A because Landlord B requires that they both be paid under the net lease if Tenant A wants to lease space in the mall. They are expenses that relate to operating the qualifying property.

    Tenant A also pays its monthly invoice for phone and internet service to its provider.

    Tenant A must pay for these services if they choose to have phone and internet service for their business. These payments are not regular instalments of operating expenses customarily charged to the tenant under the lease agreement.

    As a result, the amounts paid by Tenant A for phone and internet are not eligible rent expenses.

    Expenses such as electricity are not eligible rent expenses if the lease agreement

    • does not require a tenant to pay the amount under the lease but states only that the tenant is responsible for paying their own electricity, whether it is paid to the landlord or to a third party such as an energy distributor, or
    • says nothing about who is responsible for paying for electricity – in such a case, it is assumed that the tenant is responsible for paying the expense although this is not a requirement under the lease.

Amounts that you pay under a net lease are considered rent. You will add all of these expenses to the “Rent” field in the calculator when you calculate your subsidy amounts.

Expenses that are not eligible if you rent a qualifying property

  • tenant insurance
  • leasehold improvements
  • sales taxes (such as GST/HST and provincial sales taxes)
  • damages
  • interest or penalties on unpaid amounts
  • other special amounts

Eligible expenses if you own a qualifying property
  • Property and similar taxes
    • Includes school taxes and municipal taxes, if these are part of your property tax assessment
  • Property insurance
  • Interest on commercial mortgages for the purpose of purchasing real property

    Interest on commercial mortgages eligibility criteria

    Interest on commercial mortgage eligibility criteria

    For the interest portion of your commercial mortgage to be eligible, your mortgage amount cannot exceed the lesser of:

    • the lowest total principal amount secured by one or more mortgages on the property at any time after you acquired it

      and

    • the cost amount of the property

Expenses that are not eligible if you own a qualifying property

  • payments made on the principal amount of the mortgage
  • amounts that were paid or payable for a time that fall outside of the claim period
  • payments between non-arm’s-length and related entities
