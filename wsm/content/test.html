Testing options

Depending on what you want to find out, there are different ways to test content.

You can test content for:

  • readability and plain language
  • understanding
  • accessibility
  • task completion

Some common approaches to test your content with users are:

Cloze test
ask people to fill in the blanks with words they think are missing
First-click test
ask people where they would go to find specific information on a website to determine if they can find it on first try
Confidence test
ask people to read a piece of content and highlight in different colours what they understand, find confusing, don’t understand and didn’t read
A/B test
show people two versions of the same content with one small change and ask them what they understood (there are also other ways of doing A/B tests)
5 second test
ask people to read a piece of content and ask them to tell you what they understood after 5 seconds
Card sorting
ask people to group a number of topics under predetermined categories or ask people to group a number of topics under the categories they get to name

All of these methods can be done

  • in-person and remotely
  • quickly and inexpensively

Have a research team? Work with them to set up a usability test.

Don’t have access to professional tools or a research team who can support you? Don’t worry. You don’t need special software to do these tests. Be creative. Use paper prototyping or common collaboration tools like Google docs, slides or Trello to test content.

Don’t have access to representative users? Ask colleagues who don’t know the subject to participate in tests.

Be careful about collecting or keeping any personal data while testing content.

You can also use automated tools to:

  • check content structure
  • check the reading level of content

Interpreting data

Data can sometimes be difficult to interpret. To make sure you are making accurate and unbiased interpretations of data:

  • Use multiple sources of data to understand the problem
  • Combine qualitative (what people say) and quantitative data (insights represented as numbers)
  • Ask for help from user researchers and data analysts in your organization
  • Invite different team members to contribute their diverse readings of data
