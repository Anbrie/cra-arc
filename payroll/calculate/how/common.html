CPP, EI and income tax deductions tables

Information on how to calculate and to determine the payroll deductions for amounts you pay your employees in different pay periods.

We encourage you to take advantage of our electronic payroll deductions services. Use PDOC to quickly calculate payroll deductions for all federal, provinces and territories except Quebec. PDOC calculates payroll deductions for the most common pay periods (such as weekly or biweekly), based on exact salary figures.

Determine the CPP contribution to withhold

The annual basic exemption is built into the CPP tables.

    Previous year tables

    • Uncommon pay periods (T4008)

  2. Find the range that includes your employee's gross remuneration in the "Pay" column, include any taxable benefits

  3. Find the amount in the "CPP" column which is next to the the column "Pay"

  4. This amount is the CPP contribution that you should withhold from your employee's pay.

    You have to remit this amount and the same amount for your share of the CPP contribution.

    You stop deducting CPP when the employee reaches the maximum contribution for the year. To verify, go to Manual calculations of payroll deductions

Determine the EI premiums to withhold

You need to get the specific tax tables based on your determination of the province of employment, learn more: Determine the province of employment (POE)

  1. Open one of the following EI premiums tables that matches your employee's province of employment

    • Employment insurance (EI) premiums tables [PDF] , other than the province of Quebec

    • Quebec employment insurance (EI) premiums tables [PDF]

    Previous year tables

    Common pay periods (T4032)

  2. Find the range that includes your employee's gross remuneration in the "Insurable earnings" column, include any taxable benefits

  3. Find the amount in the "Insurable premium" column which is next to the column "Insurable earnings"

  4. This amount is the EI premium that you should withhold from your employee's pay.

    You have to remit this amount and the same amount for your share of the EI premium.

    You stop deducting EI when the employee reaches the maximum annual premium for the year. To verify, go to Manual calculations of payroll deductions

Determine the federal, provincial and territorial income tax deductions tables

What's new for 2022 - Federal income tax

For 2022, employers can use a Federal Basic Personal Amount (BPAF) of $14,398 for all employees.

The federal income tax thresholds have been indexed for 2022.

The federal Canada Employment Amount has been indexed to $1,287 for 2022.

Indexing for 2022

For 2022, the following have been updated based in the changes to the consumer price index:

  • federal income thresholds
  • personal amounts
  • Canada employment amount

The federal indexing factor for January 1, 2022 is 2.4%. The tax credits corresponding to the claim codes in the tables have been indexed accordingly.

Employees will automatically receive the indexing change, whether or not they file Form TD1, 2022 Personal Tax Credits Return.

Canada employment amount

The non-refundable Canada employment amount is built into the federal payroll deductions tables. The federal Canada employment amount is the lesser of:

  • $1,287 and
  • the individual's employment income for the year

The maximum annual non-refundable tax credit is $193.05.

Pension income is not eligible for this credit. If you are paying pension income, use the Payroll Deductions Online Calculator to find the tax deduction.

Personal amounts

The federal personal amounts for 2022 are:

Basic personal amount (maximum) - $14,398

Basic personal amount (minimum) - $12,719

You need to use the information from the Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return that was completed by your employee or your pensioner when they started their new job or they wanted to increase/reduce income tax deductions.

Learn more: Get the completed TD1 forms

What if an employe checked the box "Income from other employers or payers"

Do not deduct any federal or provincial tax, if the individual checked the box on their TD1 forms that their expected employment income from all sources will be less than their total claim amount.

However, if you know that this statement is false, you must deduct federal and provincial tax from the salary. Deduct tax according to the claim code that applies to the "13. Total claim amount" of the TD1 forms.

Explanation of claim codes

The "13. Total claim amount" reported on your employee's or pensionner's Form TD1 will determine which claim code you use. The claim amounts that correspond to the federal claim codes are not the same as the claim amounts that correspond to the provincial claim codes.

List of 2022 federal and provincial claim codes

The credits that apply to each federal and provincial claim code have been automatically changed in the tax tables by the indexing factor for the current year.

If your employee did not complete the federal and provincial TD1 forms for 2022, you continue to deduct income tax using the same claim code that you used last year.

If the federal claim code is "0" because the employee is a non-resident, the provincial claim code must also be "0." This code may also be used if the employee indicated they have more than one employer or payer at the same time and have entered "0" on the front page of Form TD1 for 2022.
