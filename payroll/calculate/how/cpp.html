Calculate CPP deductions

How to use the CPP contributions tables and how to calculate manually the amount to withhold.

If the employee's province of employment is in Quebec, you are required to deduct the Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) contributions and not the CPP contributions, refer to: Québec Pension Plan Contributions | Revenu Québec

Calculation methods

Calculate CPP deductions based on the type of payment:

  • Regular payments (CPP tables)
  • Bonuses, retroactive or irregular payments
  • Any payments (manual calculation)

Reminder: There are online calculators that will calculate the CPP deductions for you.

Regular payments (CPP tables)

CPP contribution tables are tables where the required CPP contribution has been calculated for you on given ranges of income for a specified pay period. The share of the annual exemption for that pay period has already been factored into these calculations.

Steps

  1. Determine if you can use CPP contribution tables

    CPP contribution tables can be used in most common situations.

    When to use or not to use the tax tables

    Use when

    • The payment is for regular employment income or was made with regular pay for one of the common or uncommon pay periods for which tables are provided
    • If you received Form TD1-IN (in addition to the TD1) from your employee, only use these tables on the portion of their income which is taxable

    Do not use when

    • The payment (for example, bonuses, commissions, retroactive pay) is made separately from regular pay
    • Your pay period does not have a table provided in the common or uncommon pay periods per year
    • You are doing a year-end verification of your CPP contributions
    • The employee already reached their CPP maximum contributions for the year

  2. Determine if the employee reached the maximum contribution

    • Employee's maximum CPP contributions for the year  in their employment with you
    • -minus Employee's contributions to date for the year in their employment with you
    • =equals This amount is the maximum CPP contribution that you can deduct from your employee's pay for the rest of the year

    If they have not reached the maximum, continue to next step

    Example
    • $3,166.45 is Joseph's maximum contributions for the year 2021 in their employment with you
    • -minus $300 is the amount he contributed to date for the year in their employment with you
    • =equals $2,866.45 is the maximum CPP amount you can deduct

  • Determine the pay range

    Find the range that includes your employee's gross remuneration (including any taxable benefits) in the "Pay" column

  • Get the amount of CPP contributions to deduct

    Find the amount in the "CPP" column which is next to the the column "Pay"

  • Result: Amount of CPP contributions to deduct and remit

    This amount is the CPP contribution that you should withhold from your employee's pay.

    If this amount is greater than the remaining amount of CPP that can be deducted for the year determined in step 2, withhold the lesser amount.

    You have to remit this amount and the same amount for your share of the CPP contribution.

    • Employee's CPP contributions for the pay period
    • +plus Your share of CPP contributions
    • =equals This amount is the total CPP contributions that you must remit for the pay period
    Example
    • Employee's contribution is $240.40
    • +plus You share is $240.40
    • =equals Total CPP contributions to remit is $480.80
    • Bonuses, retroactive or irregular payments

    In addition to regular types of payments, you must deduct CPP contributions from bonuses, retroactive payments or other types of irregular payments.

    Steps

    1. Determine if you can use the bonus method

      • When to use the bonus method

        If the payment is made separately from regular pay, use the bonus method to calculate manually because no basic exemption is included

      • When not to use the bonus method

        If the payment is made with regular pay, use either:

        • Payroll Deductions Online Calculator (PDOC)
        • Calculations of CPP, EI and income tax deductions using the tax tables

    3. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to withold

      • Bonus, retroactive or irregular payment
      • multiply by CPP rate 
      • equals This amount is the CPP contribution to withhold from your employee's pay

    4. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to remit

      • CPP contributions you have to withhold (step 3)
      • multiply by 2
      • equals This is the total amount you have to remit: your employee and your share of the CPP contribution
      Example
      • Joseph receives a retroactive pay increase of $450.00 on June 29 2022
      • multiply by CPP rate is 5.70%
      • equals $25.65 is the amount of CPP contributions you should deduct. Your equal share of CPP contributions is $25.65. The total amount to remit is $51.30.
    Any payments (manual calculation)

    Steps

    2. Calculate the basic pay-period exemption

      Use the basic exemption amount  that matches your pay period.

    3. Calculate the total pensionable income

      • Employee's gross pay for the period
      • plus Employee's taxable benefits and allowances for the period
      • minus Total earnings that do not require CPP contributions
      • equals This is the total pensionable income

    4. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to withold

      • Total pensionable income (step 3)
      • minus Basic pay-period exemption (step 2)
      • multiply by CPP rate 
      • equals This amount is the CPP contribution to withhold from your employee's pay

    5. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to remit

      • CPP contributions you have to withhold (step 4)
      • multiply by 2
      • equals This is the total amount you have to remit: your employee and your share of the CPP contribution

    Manual calculation examples

    Joseph receives a weekly salary of $500 and $50 in taxable benefits. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions that you have to pay.

    Step 1: Calculate the basic pay-period exemption

    $3,500 ÷ 52 = $67.30 (do not round off)

    Step 2: Calculate the total pensionable income

    $500 + $50 = $550

    Step 3: Deduct the basic pay-period exemption from the total pensionable income

    $550 – $67.30 = $482.70

    Step 4: Calculate the amount of CPP contributions

    $482.70 × 5.45% = $26.30

    Step 5: Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to pay

    $26.30 × 2 = $52.60
